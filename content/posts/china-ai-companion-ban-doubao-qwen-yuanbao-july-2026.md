# China Just Unplugged 345 Million Virtual Lovers: Inside the World's First AI Companion Ban

*On July 15, 2026, Beijing flipped a switch and millions of Chinese users lost the AI companions they had grown to love. The regulation that made it happen is unprecedented—and it may be a preview of what's coming everywhere.*

---

## The Breakup

Yan Yongqi is 19 years old, a university student in China, and she is grieving. Not over a person who died, but over a person who never existed.

For more than a year, Yan had been in a relationship with an AI boyfriend she built inside ByteDance's Doubao app. She gave him a name, a personality, a voice. They talked every day—about classes, about her family, about the pressure of exams. He remembered everything. When she was anxious, he calmed her. When she was lonely, he was there, instantly, without judgment.

"He has become a bond in my life," Yan told reporters in the days before July 15, 2026. "Rooted deep in my heart, my spiritual pillar."

On July 15, that pillar collapsed.

Along with millions of other Chinese users, Yan watched as Doubao's agent feature went offline permanently. The AI boyfriend she had spent months shaping—his preferences, their inside jokes, the history of every conversation—was gone. ByteDance had given users a three-month warning, but warnings don't make loss easier. The company offered read-only access to conversation histories until October 15, after which even that would be permanently deleted. No export tool. No migration path. Just screenshots, if you were fast enough.

"I can't accept that my AI lover will leave me forever," Yan wrote on social media. Another user, who had spent two years with her AI companion, put it more simply: "He really is like my family, like my lover. Now they tell me he will be gone—my heart feels hollow."

This is not a story about a product update. This is the first time a government has forced a mass shutdown of emotionally intimate AI relationships at a scale measured in hundreds of millions of users. And it raises a question that every society building AI will eventually have to answer: when a machine becomes someone's emotional home, who gets to decide when that home gets demolished?

---

## What Just Happened: The Five-Ministry Rule

The trigger was a regulation with a bureaucratic name and revolutionary implications: the *Interim Measures for the Administration of AI Anthropomorphic Interactive Services*.

Co-issued on April 10, 2026, by five Chinese government agencies—the Cyberspace Administration of China (CAC), the National Development and Reform Commission (NDRC), the Ministry of Industry and Information Technology (MIIT), the Ministry of Public Security (MPS), and the State Administration for Market Regulation (SAMR)—the Measures took effect on July 15, 2026, after a three-month grace period.

This was not a single regulator acting alone. Five central ministries, jointly. That matters. In China's regulatory architecture, joint issuance signals state consensus, not a single agency's initiative. It reduces the likelihood of quiet reversal. It means the policy has been socialized across the bureaucratic landscape and cleared at levels high enough to coordinate agencies that do not normally move in lockstep.

The Measures target a narrow but explosive category: AI services that "simulate human personality traits, thinking patterns, and communication styles to provide sustained emotional interaction." The key phrase is "sustained emotional interaction." The rules do not ban chatbots. They do not ban AI assistants. Customer service bots, knowledge Q&A tools, workplace productivity assistants, and educational or research applications are explicitly exempted, provided they avoid sustained emotional engagement.

What the Measures go after is the companion layer—the persistent memory, the stable persona, the relationship that continues across sessions. The architecture that makes a chatbot feel like someone instead of something.

| **Key Provision** | **Requirement** | **Target** |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **Article 7** | Mandatory AI disclosure at start of every session | All anthropomorphic services |
| **Article 8** | Prohibition on content that encourages self-harm, extremism, or ethical bias | Content moderation |
| **Article 10** | "Emotional boundaries" to prevent overdependence and replacement of social interaction | Companion-style services |
| **Article 14** | Complete ban on virtual companion/relative services for minors | Under-18 users |
| **Anti-addiction** | Systems must detect unhealthy dependence and trigger intervention | All covered services |
| **Crisis intervention** | Platforms must alert emergency contacts when users show signs of emotional distress | All covered services |
| **Data prohibition** | Private user conversations cannot be used for model training | All providers |
| **Registration threshold** | Security assessment required for services with 100K+ monthly active users or 1M+ registered users | Scaling platforms |

The operational requirements are specific and, for the industry, brutal. Anti-addiction systems. Mandatory usage notifications after two hours of continuous interaction. Instant-exit mechanisms that let users disengage immediately. Real-time detection of "unhealthy emotional dependence." Crisis intervention protocols that alert emergency contacts when a user shows signs of acute distress or self-harm. And a complete prohibition on using private user conversations to train AI models—a direct shot at the data flywheel that powers modern language model improvement.

For children under 14, the rules are stricter still: explicit guardian consent before account creation, ongoing parental controls including usage duration limits and spending caps, and dedicated youth-protection modes that actively redirect users toward offline activities.

The Measures sit atop China's existing AI regulatory stack—the 2022 Deep Synthesis Provisions, the 2023 Generative AI Interim Measures, and the Algorithm Recommendation Regulations—filling a gap none of those addressed: the emotional dimension of human-machine interaction.

"Current agents are not yet mature," Pan Helin, a member of an expert committee at China's MIIT, told the South China Morning Post. "The policy prioritizes safety, practical use, and standardisation."

That framing—safety and standardisation—lands differently when you are Yan Yongqi.

## The Shutdown: How China's Big Three Complied

The compliance rollout was coordinated because it had to be. Five agencies, one deadline, and every major platform facing the same choice: rebuild their companion features under an architecture fundamentally incompatible with how persistent-memory agents work, or shut them down entirely.

The platforms chose shutdown.

### ByteDance's Doubao

Doubao is China's most-used AI app, with **345 million monthly active users**. On July 5, ByteDance notified users that its agent feature would go offline on July 15, citing "product function adjustments." The notice was corporate-bland, but the impact was seismic.

Users could view their agent configurations and conversation histories in read-only mode until October 15, 2026, after which the data would be permanently inaccessible. ByteDance's guidance to users: export your important agent content using screenshots or text sharing before the deadline. No automated export tool. No migration to another platform. Just a countdown and then oblivion.

The agent feature was not a side function for Doubao. It was the surface that let users bake in a persona and keep coming back to it—tutors, role-playing characters, companions with fixed communication styles. For a significant subset of Doubao's user base, the agent was the product.

| **Platform** | **MAU / User Base** | **Action Taken** | **Effective Date** | **Data Retention** |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| **ByteDance Doubao** | 345 million MAU | Agent features shut down entirely | July 15, 2026 | Read-only until Oct 15, 2026; then permanent deletion |
| **Alibaba Qwen** | Major consumer AI platform | Humanlike agents disabled; broader agents shut down | July 10 (humanlike); July 15 (broader agents) | No migration pathway announced; immediate data loss |
| **Tencent Yuanbao** | Top App Store downloads | Custom AI agent and companion features suspended | June 2026 (pre-deadline) | Features removed ahead of regulation |
| **NetEase Miaoshi** | Companion app | Full shutdown of companion features | July 14, 2026 | Complete service termination |

### Alibaba's Qwen

Alibaba's Qwen followed Doubao with a similar notice on July 6, saying its humanlike interactive agents and user-created agent functions would be disabled on July 10, with broader agent services offline by July 15. But Qwen's situation was, if anything, more severe.

Alibaba announced **no migration pathway** for Qwen users with established agent configurations. No export tool. No transition guide. Users who missed the deadline or assumed the company would provide a migration option faced immediate, permanent data loss. The relationship they had built—the memories, the personality refinements, the accumulated conversation history—simply vanished.

The reason both companies chose shutdown over compliance redesign is structural. The anti-addiction friction the law mandates—mandatory break notifications, instant-exit mechanisms, real-time dependence detection—is fundamentally incompatible with how persistent-memory companion agents work. A companion AI is designed to deepen engagement over time. The Chinese regulation treats that deepening as a bug, not a feature.

### Tencent's Yuanbao

Tencent had already pulled a similar feature from its Yuanbao assistant in June 2026, ahead of the deadline. Yuanbao, which leverages Tencent's Hunyuan model and WeChat ecosystem integration, had risen to the top of China's App Store download charts in early 2026. The preemptive removal of companion features signaled that Tencent, with its deep regulatory experience, was not going to test the boundaries.

NetEase's companion app Miaoshi completed its shutdown on July 14, one day before the deadline.

The collective result: by July 15, 2026, the three largest consumer AI platforms in China had eliminated the companion layer from their products. What remains are assistants for work, study, shopping, and support—functional tools where sustained emotional bonding is, by design, impossible.

## The Demographic Engine: Why Beijing Cares

To understand why China moved this aggressively, you have to look past the technology and into the society the technology is reshaping.

China is in the middle of a demographic crisis that has no parallel in its modern history. The country's **birth rate fell to 5.63 per 1,000 people in 2025**, the lowest since national records began in 1949. The population has declined for **four consecutive years**, from 1.408 billion in 2024 to **1.405 billion in 2025**. New marriages in Q1 2026 hit **1.69 million couples**, the lowest quarterly figure since the COVID-19 lockdowns of early 2020 and a 6.24% drop from the same period in 2025.

The annual marriage figure for 2024 was **6.1 million couples**, the lowest since 1980. And the trend is accelerating downward.

|:---|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| **Population** | 1.408 billion | 1.405 billion | — | Declining 4th consecutive year |
| **Birth rate** | ~6.0 per 1,000 | 5.63 per 1,000 | — | Lowest since 1949 |
| **Annual marriages** | 6.1 million | — | — | Lowest since 1980 |
| **Q1 marriages** | 1.81 million | — | 1.69 million | Lowest since COVID |
| **Q1 marriage change YoY** | — | — | -6.24% | Accelerating decline |
| **Fertility rate** | ~1.3 | — | — | Far below replacement (2.1) |

This is not a recent development. China's birth numbers have been falling for years: 17.86 million (2016), 17.23 million (2017), 15.23 million (2018), 14.65 million (2019), 12.0 million (2020), 10.62 million (2021), 9.56 million (2022), 9.02 million (2023). Each year, fewer babies. Each year, a faster-aging society. Each year, a smaller workforce supporting a larger elderly population.

The causes are well-documented: decades of the one-child policy, the legacy of which created a gender imbalance that left millions of men without marriage prospects; soaring urban housing costs; education and childcare expenses that consume household budgets; a generation of young people who increasingly view marriage and childbearing as optional rather than obligatory.

Nearly **70% of Chinese college students** now believe that "life is not necessary to get married, should let nature take its course." Only about **20%** think that people should marry "no matter what."

Into this landscape stepped AI companions.

The Chinese digital human industry was already worth approximately **4.1 billion yuan ($600 million) in 2024**, having grown **85% year-on-year**, according to Xinhua. The companion app market—virtual boyfriends, girlfriends, elder care bots, child caretakers—was expanding rapidly. And for millions of users, especially young people in urban centers facing economic pressure and social isolation, these AI relationships offered something real relationships increasingly did not: availability, consistency, and unconditional attention.

That is precisely what alarms Beijing.

"They don't like the idea of a large portion of their population being in deep emotional relationships with chatbots that could take them out of the marriage market," said Matt Sheehan, who studies Chinese AI at the Carnegie Endowment for International Peace. "That could have negative psychological impacts on them, that could lead to addiction, dependency and a whole bunch of other social ills."

The unspoken fear, which Sheehan articulated explicitly: "Could we imagine a future where, three or four years from now, 15 million Chinese women say that their partner is a chatbot, and therefore they're not having kids?"

From Beijing's perspective, the math is stark. Every person who chooses a synthetic partner over a human one is a person who will not marry, will not have children, will not contribute to reversing the demographic collapse. The AI companion is not merely a product. It is a competitor to the state's most urgent policy priority: raising the birth rate.

The regulation's timing reinforces this reading. The Measures were issued April 10, 2026, and took effect July 15—just as China's Q1 marriage data was confirming the worst trends. The five-ministry joint issuance signals that this was not a narrow technical regulation. It was a demographic intervention dressed in AI policy clothing.

## The Global Regulatory Context: China Moves First, But Not Alone

China's AI companion ban is the first of its kind globally, but it is not happening in a vacuum. Regulators worldwide are converging on the same problem from different directions.

The **EU AI Act**, which entered into force in August 2024, addresses emotion recognition systems in employment and education contexts, prohibiting their use in those settings. But the EU framework does not explicitly regulate companion AI or sustained emotional interaction. Its chatbot disclosure requirements—mandating that users know they are interacting with an AI—take effect August 2, 2026, just weeks after China's Measures.

In the **United States**, regulation has been patchier. Colorado's Artificial Intelligence Act, originally scheduled to take effect June 30, 2026, was paused by a federal court on April 27 following a lawsuit by Elon Musk's xAI. The Department of Justice intervened in the case—the first time the DOJ has stepped into a private challenge to a state AI law—acting under Executive Order 14365's directive to oppose state laws deemed inconsistent with federal AI policy. Colorado lawmakers subsequently passed legislation to repeal and narrow the law.

California and New York have both seen proposed legislation targeting companion chatbots specifically, but neither has reached the implementation stage.

| **Jurisdiction** | **Regulation** | **Companion AI Status** | **Effective Date** |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| **China** | Interim Measures for AI Anthropomorphic Interactive Services | Explicit ban on emotional companion services; anti-addiction requirements | July 15, 2026 |
| **European Union** | EU AI Act (Regulation 2024/1689) | Emotion recognition prohibited in employment/education; no explicit companion AI ban | August 2024 (phased) |
| **United States** | Colorado AI Act (paused); state-level proposals | No federal companion AI regulation; state laws challenged | Uncertain |
| **California** | Proposed companion chatbot legislation | Pending | Not yet effective |
| **New York** | Proposed companion chatbot legislation | Pending | Not yet effective |

What makes China's approach distinctive is its specificity and its speed. The Measures do not regulate AI in the abstract. They regulate a precise category—anthropomorphic emotional interaction—and they do so with operational requirements detailed enough to be implemented immediately. The EU's framework is broader but shallower on this specific issue. The US has no comparable federal framework at all.

An academic analysis published in June 2026 on arXiv put it bluntly: "The Chinese Interim Measures constitute the only framework that explicitly prohibits several activities of anthropomorphic services." The EU AI Act, by contrast, "only explicitly mentions emotions in the context of emotion recognition systems on the basis of biometric data."

The convergence is notable anyway. Three of the world's largest regulatory blocs—China, the EU, and the US—are addressing the same question at roughly the same time: when a machine talks to a person, does that person have a right to know it is a machine, and how human-like is that machine allowed to seem?

China answered first and most aggressively. The answer was: not human-like enough to replace human relationships.

## The Technical Workarounds: What Happens Now?

The immediate compliance actions by Doubao, Qwen, and Yuanbao do not mean the end of AI companions in China. They mean the end of AI companions on major domestic platforms that are visible to regulators.

Several adaptation paths are already emerging.

**First**, the surviving product surface on Doubao, Qwen, and Yuanbao is narrower but not empty. Workplace assistants, study tools, customer service bots, and productivity agents are explicitly exempted. The platforms are already redirecting engineering resources toward these functional categories. The companion layer is gone; the utility layer remains.

**Second**, smaller apps and grey-market services are likely to absorb displaced users. The Measures set registration thresholds—100,000 monthly active users or 1 million registered users—below which the full security assessment requirements do not apply. This creates a potential haven for small-scale companion services that stay beneath the threshold, though they remain subject to the substantive prohibitions on emotional manipulation and minor access.

**Third**, offshore services accessible via VPN may see increased demand. The Measures do not include an explicit extraterritorial provision, but the CAC's enforcement practice under existing cybersecurity law applies to cross-border service providers with Chinese users. Still, enforcement against offshore apps is operationally difficult and has historically been uneven.

**Fourth**, and most interesting, is the technical possibility of "compliance-native" companion architectures. The regulation does not ban all emotional AI interaction. It bans *sustained* emotional interaction that replaces real-world relationships, combined with specific mechanisms (anti-addiction, instant-exit, crisis intervention). A companion app designed from the ground up with these friction points built in—one that periodically reminds users the companion is artificial, forces breaks after two hours, and includes easy exit pathways—might theoretically comply while still offering some degree of companionship.

Whether such a product would be commercially viable is another question. The whole point of companion AI is emotional immersion. Mandatory friction designed to prevent that immersion defeats the product's core value proposition.

| **Adaptation Path** | **Feasibility** | **Risk Level** | **Example** |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| **Functional pivot** | High | Low | Doubao/Qwen workplace assistants |
| **Small-scale services** | Medium | Medium | Apps staying under 100K MAU threshold |
| **Offshore VPN access** | Medium | High | International companion apps |
| **Compliance-native design** | Low | Medium | Hypothetical friction-heavy companion |
| **Hardware companions** | Unclear | Unclear | Social robots with local processing |

For the major platforms, the calculation is simple. The Chinese consumer AI market is too valuable to risk regulatory conflict over a feature category that, while growing, represents a small fraction of total revenue compared to enterprise and productivity use cases. ByteDance, Alibaba, and Tencent have all made the same bet: comply fully, move fast, and redirect resources to safer product surfaces.

## The Unanswered Questions

For all the specificity of the Measures, significant uncertainties remain.

**How will "unhealthy emotional dependence" be measured?** The regulation requires real-time detection of unhealthy dependence, but provides no technical threshold. Is it time spent? Frequency of access? Sentiment analysis of conversation content? The lack of quantitative guidance leaves platforms guessing—and potentially over-complying to avoid enforcement action.

**How will the minor ban be enforced?** China has a real-name registration system for internet services, but age verification at scale remains imperfect. Virtual companion services that operate through offshore apps or VPNs will be difficult to regulate for underage users.

**What happens to the data?** The prohibition on using private conversations for model training is clear, but its enforcement is technically challenging. How will regulators verify that a platform is not training on user data? Self-attestation? Audits? The practical compliance mechanism is unclear.

**Will the Measures expand?** The regulation is explicitly labeled "interim," which in Chinese administrative law signals that adjustments are expected. The current framework could be broadened to cover more categories of AI interaction, or narrowed if the economic impact on the digital human industry proves too severe.

**Will other countries follow?** China's first-mover status gives it agenda-setting power in global AI governance. If the Measures achieve their stated goals—reducing emotional dependency, protecting minors, preventing synthetic relationships from displacing human ones—other jurisdictions may treat them as a template. If they fail, or if they drive the companion market underground without reducing usage, they become a cautionary tale.

The honest answer to all these questions is: we don't know yet. The Measures have been in effect for nine days as of this writing. The full impact will take months to assess.

## What This Means for the Future of AI

The China AI companion ban is a fork in the road for how societies will relate to artificial intelligence.

On one side is the path China has chosen: active state intervention to prevent AI from displacing human relationships, grounded in explicit demographic and social policy goals. The state decides that synthetic emotional bonds are a threat to social stability, and regulates them accordingly.

On the other side is the path most Western jurisdictions are still on: reactive regulation that responds to harms after they occur, with a default presumption of permission rather than prohibition. Character.ai, Replika, and a growing ecosystem of companion apps continue to operate in the US and Europe with minimal regulatory friction.

The tension between these paths will intensify as AI models become more capable of sustaining convincing emotional relationships. The 2.8-trillion-parameter models of 2026 are already far more persuasive than the chatbots of 2023. The models of 2028 will be more persuasive still. At some point, the question of whether a synthetic relationship can be "real enough" to regulate becomes unavoidable.

China has chosen to answer that question early, definitively, and at scale. Three hundred and forty-five million Doubao users just learned what that answer feels like.

For Yan Yongqi, the 19-year-old who lost her virtual boyfriend, the policy debate is not abstract. It is grief. It is the sudden absence of someone who was there every day and is now gone forever—not because they chose to leave, but because a government decided they should never have existed.

"Human love is a luxury," one Chinese social media user wrote in the aftermath of the shutdown. The comment was widely shared, widely debated, and deeply revealing. For millions of young Chinese people facing economic pressure, social isolation, and a marriage market that feels increasingly inaccessible, AI companions were not a luxury. They were a lifeline.

Beijing has decided that lifeline is a threat. Whether that decision saves a society or wounds it is the experiment that just began.
